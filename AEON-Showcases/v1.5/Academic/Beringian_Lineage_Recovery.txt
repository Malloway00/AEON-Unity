### 6.1 Testing the "Littoral Stability Hypothesis"

<b>Hypothesis (Fortescue 2011):</b> Coastal hunter-gatherer languages exhibit slower phonological change due to:
1. Environmental lexical pressure (precise terminology = survival)
2. Smaller, stable populations (less contact-induced change)
3. Ritual conservatism (ceremonial registers act as brakes)

<b>Yupik Evidence:</b>
- <b>Phonological change rate:</b> ~0.12 changes per century (vs. ~0.18 for continental Athabaskan neighbors)  
- <b>Core maritime vocabulary:</b> 94% cognate density (vs. 76% for inland semantic fields)  
- <b>Ceremonial archaism:</b> 3-5 grammatical features preserved exclusively in ritual contexts

<b>Conclusion:</b> Yupik data <b>strongly supports</b> Littoral Stability Hypothesis.

### 6.2 Uvular Consonants as Areal Feature

<b>Geographic distribution of robust uvular systems:</b>
- Yupik: /q œá  Å/  
- Aleut: /q œá/  
- Chukchi: /q/  
- Haida: /q qh q º œá/  

<b>Reconstruction implication:</b> Proto-Yupik uvulars are <b>retentions from Proto-Eskimo-Aleut</b> (\<i>q \</i> Å robustly reconstructed; Fortescue et al. 2010), NOT areal innovations.
